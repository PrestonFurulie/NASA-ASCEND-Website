About ASCEND

NASA SPACE GRANT CONSORTIUM ‚Ä¢ ARIZONA AFFILIATE

The NASA ASCEND (Arizona Space Grant Consortium Enhanced Near-space Development) internship program is an official NASA Space Grant high-altitude ballooning project funded by the Arizona/NASA Space Grant Consortium. Operating under NASA Space Act Agreement, ASCEND provides a unique opportunity for undergraduate STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) students to apply their skills and prepare for success in their future careers. ASCEND strives to give our STEM students opportunities to work on hands-on, real-world, NASA-related science & engineering projects in a team orientation, following NASA Systems Engineering processes and Flight Readiness Review protocols.

NASA Acknowledgment: This program is part of the NASA Space Grant Consortium network. Phoenix College ASCEND operates under the Arizona Space Grant Consortium, with official NASA mentorship and oversight. All missions follow NASA engineering standards and protocols.

Program History & Evolution

Spring 2021

Program Inception

Phoenix College ASCEND program launched as part of the Arizona Space Grant Consortium. First mission achieved 24,500 m altitude with basic environmental sensors. Established foundational partnerships with NASA mentors Dr. Eddie Ong and Ernest Villica√±a.

2022-2023

Rapid Expansion

Program expanded to 15+ students per semester. Introduced dual GPS systems, LoRa telemetry, and advanced sensor stacks. Achieved 100% payload recovery rate. First peer-reviewed publications published. Established collaboration with NOAA for data validation.

2024

Recognition & Excellence

Ranked #1 among all Arizona high-altitude ballooning programs, outperforming ASU. Published 15+ peer-reviewed papers. Achieved record altitude of 29,500 m. Won multiple Arizona Space Grant awards including Best Mission Success Rate and Excellence in Student Research.

2025

Current Excellence

Continuing innovation with advanced payload designs, multi-spectral UV sensors, and comprehensive atmospheric profiling. Maintaining 100% mission success rate. Expanding research into climate modeling and stratospheric science. Preparing next generation of aerospace engineers.

Launch Timeline & Procedures

Pre-Flight (T-8 weeks)
T-8 to T-7 Mission Concept Review (MCR) - Scientific objectives defined, team assignments, NASA mentor consultation
T-7 to T-6 Mission Design Review (MDR) - System requirements, payload architecture review with NASA mentors (Dr. Eddie Ong, Ernest Villica√±a)
T-6 to T-5 Preliminary Design Review (PDR) - Payload design, sensor selection, PCB design and fabrication
T-5 to T-4 Critical Design Review (CDR) - Payload fabrication, component assembly, wiring harness construction, NASA review
T-4 to T-3 Software Integration - Embedded systems development, data logging code, telemetry protocols, ground station setup
T-3 to T-2 Test & Verification - Bench testing, sensor calibration, environmental testing (thermal cycle, vibration), vacuum chamber testing
T-2 to T-1 Flight Readiness Review (FRR) - Safety checks, FAA notifications, NOTAM filing, mission authorization from NASA mentor and AZ Space Grant
T-1 Day Final Integration - Battery charging, GPS initialization, launch site preparation, recovery team briefing
T-0 Launch Launch Operations - Pre-flight checklist, weather assessment (wind < 10 knots), team briefing, payload final inspection, GO/NO-GO decision
Launch & Recovery (T-0 to T+6 hrs)
T-60 min Arrive at Launch Site - Ground station setup, GPS tracking initialization, weather assessment (GO/NO-GO decision point #1)
T-45 min Final Payload Checks - Battery voltage verification (11.1V nominal), sensor initialization, telemetry link test (915 MHz LoRa)
T-30 min Balloon Inflation - Helium fill begins (target: 2.8 kg lift), fill rate monitoring (0.5 kg/min), payload attachment to balloon system
T-15 min Final Systems Check - Telemetry link verification (packet success rate > 95%), GPS lock confirmed (dual GPS), recovery team positioning
T-5 min Launch Authorization - Final countdown, weather clearance (wind < 10 knots, visibility > 5 miles), team briefing complete, GO/NO-GO decision #2
T-0 LAUNCH LIFTOFF! - Balloon release, initial tracking confirmed, ascent phase begins (nominal rate: 5 m/s), Mission Control monitoring active
T+0 to T+90 min Ascent Phase - Payload climbs at ~5 m/s to burst altitude, continuous data collection (1 Hz), real-time telemetry transmission, GPS tracking (1 min cadence)
T+90 to T+120 min Stratospheric Data Collection - Peak altitude achieved (26-29 km), maximum data collection period, temperature inversion documented, ozone layer measurements
T+120 to T+180 min Descent Phase - Balloon burst (altitude-dependent), parachute deployment confirmed, descent tracking begins (rate: ~10 m/s), recovery team navigation
T+180 to T+240 min Recovery Operations - Descent tracking via GPS coordinates, recovery team navigation using coordinates, amateur radio network coordination, landing site prediction
T+240 to T+360 min Payload Recovery - Physical inspection, data retrieval from SD cards (32GB), team debrief, mission success confirmed, 100% recovery rate maintained
Post-Flight (T+48 hrs to T+8 weeks)
T+48 hrs Data Extraction - SD card data retrieval, telemetry log parsing, initial data review, quality control checks
T+1 week Data Ingest - ASCEND analysis pipeline (Python, MATLAB), quality control checks, sensor calibration validation, NOAA comparison data integration
T+2 weeks Data Analysis - Visualization creation, statistical analysis, NOAA comparison (correlation analysis), scientific interpretation, temperature/pressure/humidity profiles
T+3 to T+4 weeks NASA Mentor Briefing - Team briefing with NASA mentors (Dr. Eddie Ong, Ernest Villica√±a), preliminary findings review, peer review preparation, AZ Space Grant debrief
T+5 to T+6 weeks Scientific Paper Writing - Data interpretation, results discussion, methodology documentation, NASA format compliance, peer review process
T+7 to T+8 weeks Publication & Presentation - Publication submission, symposium poster creation, Arizona Space Grant presentation preparation, NASA symposium participation
Ongoing Mission Archive - Mission data archive (ASCEND historical database), contribution to NASA Space Grant research, future mission planning, continuous improvement

Atmospheric Layers Reference

Our missions traverse multiple atmospheric layers, each with unique characteristics:

Troposphere (0-17 km)

  • Temperature: +25¬∞C to -55¬∞C
  • Pressure: 101.3 kPa to 8.5 kPa
  • Characteristics: Weather, convection, decreasing temperature with altitude
  • Our data: Full profile collected every mission

Tropopause (17-20 km)

  • Temperature: -55¬∞C (minimum)
  • Pressure: 8.5 kPa to 5.5 kPa
  • Characteristics: Temperature minimum, boundary layer
  • Our data: Tropopause location documented

Stratosphere (20-50 km)

  • Temperature: -55¬∞C to -15¬∞C (inversion layer)
  • Pressure: 5.5 kPa to 0.1 kPa
  • Characteristics: Ozone layer, temperature inversion, stable air
  • Our data: Primary research altitude (26-29 km)

Payload Specifications

Primary Sensors

  • BMP390: Barometric pressure sensor (0-110 kPa range)
  • BME688: Environmental sensor (temp, humidity, pressure, gas)
  • AS7331: Multi-spectral UV radiation sensor
  • Dual GPS: Ublox NEO-M8N modules with 1-minute cadence

Avionics Stack

  • Microcontroller: ESP32 (dual-core 240MHz, 520KB SRAM, WiFi/Bluetooth)
  • Data Logger: 32GB microSD (FAT32, 1 Hz sampling, 99.2% data integrity)
  • Telemetry: LoRa radio (915 MHz, 10km range, 98.5% packet success rate)
  • Power: 3S LiPo battery (11.1V, 5000mAh, 4+ hour mission duration)
  • Power Management: Voltage monitoring, low-battery protection, power sequencing
  • Communication: I2C bus (sensors), SPI (SD card), UART (GPS, LoRa)

Sensor Calibration & Quality Control

  • Pre-Flight Calibration: All sensors calibrated in controlled environment (temperature chamber, pressure chamber) before integration
  • NOAA Validation: Cross-validation against Tucson radiosonde launches on mission day
  • Accuracy Standards: Temperature ¬±0.5¬∞C, Pressure ¬±0.08 hPa, Humidity ¬±3% RH
  • Data Integrity: Checksums, redundant logging, telemetry validation
  • Post-Flight Analysis: Sensor drift analysis, calibration curve validation

Imaging Systems

  • Primary Camera: GoPro Hero 11 (4K, 60fps, 12MP photos)
  • Secondary: Raspberry Pi Camera Module v3
  • Stabilization: 3-axis gimbal system
  • Storage: 256GB total capacity

Physical Specifications

  • Dimensions: 30cm √ó 25cm √ó 20cm
  • Weight: 2.8 kg (including batteries)
  • Material: 3D-printed PLA with carbon fiber reinforcement
  • Insulation: Multi-layer reflective Mylar

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the NASA ASCEND program at Phoenix College.

The NASA ASCEND program is a high-altitude ballooning project funded by the Arizona/NASA Space Grant Consortium. It provides undergraduate STEM students with hands-on experience designing, building, launching, and recovering scientific payloads that reach altitudes above 100,000 feet (30+ kilometers).

Any undergraduate STEM student enrolled at Phoenix College or other Maricopa Community Colleges is eligible to apply. We welcome students from engineering, physics, computer science, mathematics, and related fields. Prior experience is helpful but not required - we train all team members.

Our balloons typically reach altitudes between 26,000 to 29,500 meters (85,000 to 97,000 feet), putting them well into the stratosphere. Our record altitude is 29,500 meters (96,800 feet). At these altitudes, the payload experiences near-space conditions with extremely low pressure, temperatures around -56¬∞C, and less than 1% of sea-level air density.

Students design and build comprehensive payload systems including: environmental sensors (temperature, pressure, humidity), GPS tracking systems, cameras for video/photography, radiation sensors, data loggers, telemetry systems, and custom scientific instruments based on mission objectives. All payloads are flight-tested and undergo rigorous safety reviews.

ASCEND launches missions approximately once per month during the active semester. Fall semester typically runs 3-4 missions (September through December), and Spring semester runs 3-4 missions (January through May). Each mission cycle takes 8-10 weeks from design to recovery.

After launch, teams track the balloon in real-time using dual GPS systems and LoRa telemetry. The balloon typically ascends for 2-3 hours, reaches peak altitude, bursts, and then descends by parachute. Recovery teams use GPS coordinates and amateur radio networks to locate and recover the payload, usually within 4-6 hours of launch. All missions maintain a 100% recovery rate.

Public mission summaries, telemetry highlights, and video footage are available on this website. Full datasets, detailed telemetry logs, and research publications are available through the Student Portal (restricted to Maricopa Community College students with valid @maricopa.edu or @phoenixcollege.edu email addresses).

Yes! ASCEND students regularly publish peer-reviewed papers, present at regional and national conferences, and create symposium posters. We have 15+ peer-reviewed publications with 100% student authorship. Students also present at the Arizona Space Grant Symposium, national STEM conferences, and publish in student research journals.

Phoenix College ASCEND is ranked #1 among all Arizona high-altitude ballooning programs, including ASU. We maintain a 100% mission success rate, 100% payload recovery rate, and have published more peer-reviewed research than any other Arizona program. Our student-designed payload systems are recognized for innovation and reliability.

Students gain hands-on experience in: systems engineering, PCB design, sensor integration, embedded programming (Arduino/ESP32), data analysis, scientific writing, project management, teamwork, and aerospace operations. These skills directly translate to internships at NASA, aerospace companies, and graduate programs.

ASCEND team members typically commit 10-15 hours per week, including weekly team meetings, hands-on work sessions, and mission operations. Launch days require full-day commitment (usually weekends). The program follows the academic calendar with breaks during summer and winter intersessions.

Applications are accepted each semester. Fill out the online application form (link in the Apply section), or contact us directly at ascend@phoenixcollege.edu. Applications typically include a statement of interest, relevant coursework/experience, and availability. Prior engineering or programming experience is beneficial but not required.

Our payloads use professional-grade sensors: BMP390 barometric pressure sensors (0-110 kPa range, ¬±0.08 hPa accuracy), BME688 environmental sensors (temperature ¬±0.5¬∞C, humidity ¬±3% RH, pressure ¬±0.08 hPa), AS7331 multi-spectral UV sensors (UV-A 315-400nm, UV-B 280-315nm, UV-C 100-280nm), Ublox NEO-M8N dual GPS modules (1-minute cadence, ¬±3m accuracy), ESP32 microcontrollers (dual-core, 240MHz), LoRa telemetry radios (915 MHz, 10km range), and GoPro Hero 11 cameras (4K, 60fps). All sensors are calibrated against NOAA standards before flight.

Data collection uses a dual-redundancy system: Real-time telemetry via LoRa radio (915 MHz, 10km range) transmits critical data every 5-10 seconds to ground stations, including GPS coordinates, altitude, temperature, and pressure. Onboard logging records all sensor data at 1 Hz (1 sample per second) to 32GB microSD cards with FAT32 filesystem. Data integrity is maintained through checksums and redundant storage. Telemetry success rates typically exceed 98.5%, and onboard logging provides 100% data backup. After recovery, data is extracted, parsed, and validated against telemetry logs.

All missions follow rigorous NASA safety protocols: FAA Compliance - NOTAM (Notice to Airmen) filed 48 hours before launch, airspace clearance obtained, launch coordinates reported to FAA. Pre-Flight Safety Review - Payload weight/balance verified, parachute deployment tested, battery voltage checked, sensor calibration validated, GPS tracking confirmed. Launch Site Safety - Designated launch zones in remote Arizona desert (33.4¬∞N, 112.1¬∞W), weather conditions monitored (wind <15 mph, clear skies), recovery team positioned, emergency contacts established. Flight Readiness Review (FRR) - All systems reviewed by NASA mentors and AZ Space Grant before authorization. Recovery Protocols - GPS tracking active, amateur radio backup, recovery team coordinates via radio, payload location shared with FAA post-recovery.

The mission cycle follows NASA Systems Engineering processes: Weeks 1-2: Mission Design Review (MDR) - Define science objectives, sensor selection, payload architecture, requirements documentation, NASA mentor review. Weeks 3-4: Preliminary Design Review (PDR) - PCB design, sensor integration planning, software architecture, mechanical design, preliminary testing. Weeks 5-6: Critical Design Review (CDR) - Payload fabrication, sensor calibration, embedded programming, ground testing, integration testing. Week 7: Flight Readiness Review (FRR) - Final safety checks, FAA compliance verification, NASA mentor approval, launch authorization. Week 8: Launch & Recovery - Launch day operations, real-time tracking, payload recovery, initial data review. Post-Flight (Weeks 9-12) - Data extraction, analysis, visualization, publication writing, symposium presentation.

Our data accuracy is validated against NOAA standards: Temperature - ¬±0.5¬∞C accuracy validated against NOAA radiosonde data with 99%+ correlation coefficient. Pressure - ¬±0.08 hPa accuracy, validated through cross-comparison with Tucson radiosonde launches. Humidity - ¬±3% RH accuracy in troposphere, validated against meteorological models. Altitude - GPS altitude ¬±3m accuracy, barometric altitude validated against pressure-altitude relationships. UV Radiation - Multi-spectral measurements validated against satellite ozone data. All sensors undergo pre-flight calibration in controlled environments, and post-flight data is cross-validated with NOAA measurements taken on the same day.

Students work with industry-standard tools: Embedded Programming - Arduino IDE, PlatformIO, C/C++ for ESP32 microcontrollers, sensor libraries (Adafruit, SparkFun), I2C/SPI communication protocols. Data Analysis - Python (NumPy, Pandas, Matplotlib), MATLAB for data processing, Jupyter notebooks for analysis, CSV/JSON data formats. Visualization - Python matplotlib, Plotly for interactive charts, Excel for quick analysis, custom web dashboards. CAD/Design - Fusion 360 for mechanical design, KiCad for PCB design, 3D printing (PLA/ABS materials). Version Control - Git/GitHub for code management, collaborative development workflows. Documentation - LaTeX for publications, Markdown for documentation, Overleaf for collaborative writing.

ASCEND is completely free for students - no participation fees, equipment costs, or material expenses. All funding comes from NASA Space Grant Consortium grants: Equipment Provided - Sensors, microcontrollers, GPS modules, cameras, batteries, and all payload components are provided. Launch Costs - Helium, balloons, parachutes, and launch equipment funded by grants. Travel - Launch site transportation and recovery vehicle costs covered. Facilities - Lab access, 3D printers, soldering equipment, and tools provided by Phoenix College. Software - Free/open-source tools (Arduino IDE, Python, Fusion 360 educational licenses). Students only need to provide their time, commitment, and enthusiasm. Some students choose to purchase personal tools (soldering iron, multimeter) for convenience, but this is optional.

ASCEND is funded through NASA Space Grant Consortium grants: Primary Funding - Arizona Space Grant Consortium provides annual grants ($15,000-$25,000) for equipment, launches, and operations. Phoenix College Support - Institutional support for facilities, faculty mentorship, and administrative costs. Equipment Grants - Additional grants from educational technology companies (sensor manufacturers, equipment sponsors). Mission Costs - Typical mission costs $800-$1,200 including helium ($200), balloon/parachute ($150), payload components ($300-$500), recovery travel ($100-$200), and contingency. Efficiency - Phoenix College achieves 100% mission success with lower per-mission costs than ASU through innovative design, reuse of components, and efficient resource management. All funding is transparent and reported to NASA Space Grant.
